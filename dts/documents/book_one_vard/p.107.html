108 March the 31, 1651 . was his blacke munday. the strange

pain which seized on my necke portended this Sadd loss.

Great, & infinite, was the search by thousands, of People

from that time till wednesday following , his body not being found

till on that day, & about the time when he was lost.

And then one of those men which was a witness against

him, was the first, which discovered his body, it being fallen

into a poole near Cattericke Bridge , a bove a mile from

the place he was drowned. John Plummer the mans name.

After they had drawne him up, care was taken of his

body, which was as sweet & comely, in all parts, as in his life,

except one bruise on the nose which was thought to be don

when he fell, upon some great stone, there being abund-

ance in that wathplace: Allbeit he was an Excellent swimer

yet was it not the Lords pleasure, that it did him any help to

be saved thereby. The Corps was laid at Thomson s

on Cattericke brigg , that night because it was deemed the

bringing him to Hipswell , would, too much have agravated

my mothers excessive sorrow, & indangered her life also.

He was carried by coach, to Kirklington in the company

of all the Gentry in that part of Yorkeshire . with a greater

Lamentation, & sorrow, then was for any, with in the memory

of man at his funeralls.

He was buried in Kirklington Church , near Sir Christoper

Wandesforde Tombe, my Great Grandfather, Mr Siddall

preaching his funeral sermon, as I take it, and with as much

solemnity, as those times & such a sudden accident could

admitt. And this is the true relation of his death, of the fall

of this stately Ceadar of our wood, our staffe to my dear fathers

Family, whose death, cannot be spoken of with out tears.
